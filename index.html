Few Words About Us

For our project “Suburban Soul” we decided to revitalize the land around the Plano Market Square Mall on the intersection of K Avenue and Spring Creek Parkway. To keep the mall aesthetic while adding a more natural and modern touch we decided to go for a half park half outdoor mall renovation of the land.

Sustainability

In line with Plano’s future goals we are taking the initiative of moving our city to a renewable future by using solar energy to reduce our dependence on harmful fossil fuels. Tesla PowerPacks will be charged using 162,000 square feet of roof mounted solar panels and supply clean, renewable energy throughout the park. In addition to this our environmental center will teach visitors about the importance of biodegradable single use plastics, their local ecosystems, and how to reduce their carbon footprint.

Full Essay

In an article titled Rejecting Consumerism Can Help The Environment, Wendee Holtcamp discusses her interactions with Compactor ideology and its effects on waste production. Compactor ideology is an extreme form of reducing one’s carbon footprint by vowing to never buy new or at least attempting to buy second hand to reduce the waste and pollutants it takes to create new products. Holtcamp believes that the United States creates a lot of waste due to its consumerist economy and seek to reduce her own waste, tried being a Compactor for a month. Having a consumerist economy, the U.S. relies on the consumer market, buying and selling new products to stimulate the GDP. According to an economics professor that was questioned by Holtcamp, if the U.S. economy’s success relies on growing GDP, then buying second-hand goods in Compactor fashion will negatively affect the economy as less stuff will be produced. Because of the professor's answer, she proposed to redefine a strong economy so that it doesn’t just measure the health of financial markets, but that it should also consider the health of natural resources as well.

Throughout the essay, the author kept a passive tone. She explains that American consumers create great amounts of waste which should change, however, she understands that she is a part of that problem, hence using words like “we” and “us” when discussing the actions of consumers. She is also trying to be more eco-friendly herself by following the way of Compactors and so she has experience of both sides of the consumer and the Compactor allowing her to be more analytical of the advantages and disadvantages with less bias which is where she comes to her conclusion that is a somewhat middle ground.

Holtcamp connects with the average middle-class consumer by beginning her essay by telling her story about buying things at the store with her kids. She recounts “I visit a Hallmark store with moy kids. The 75 percent-off rack draws me in… ‘Look at these cute penguins,’ I say, showing them to my kids. My 10-year-old son, Sam, picks one up. ‘Cool. They poop candy.’ I pay and leave the store…” showing a middle-class budget having enough money to spend on toys and non-essentials yet being frugal with the clearance aisle.

The author argues that buying second-hand reduces the environmental impact we (humanity) have on the Earth. She elaborates saying, “What’s more, each new widget is designed to wear out or otherwise fade into obsolescence, so we’ll have almost no choice but to buy more and more.” I think a large bias is in this statement. Survivorship bias. The reason we think products today don’t hold up to the products from yesteryear, is that we only have the products which made it to today to judge against them. We don’t remember those that came and went through time unceremoniously.

Getting at the main point of the essay, products today have vastly improved since the writing of this article in 2007 alongside the creation of laws to mitigate products that are single use, but non-essential. New standards have been created to help with pollution such as, the Save our Seas act of 2018, a law in California banning single-use plastic straws in full-service restaurants. Steps like these are helping mitigate the impacts of pollution from consumerism at the source, businesses. Policies like this are what make changes in the environment. Buying second-hand hurts the economy and punishes business while giving them no recourse to correct.

Another issue the author brings forth is that the measurement for GDP has little regard for the nations resources and reuse of materials. While we are not able to offer a modification of economics, our space will support the compactor ideology by having services for recycling and up-cycling. This will give support to the community by allowing families to donate items in good condition, but are no longer used, can be materials for new goods or be resold instead of going to a landfill. The store will be a dedicated part of our site run by us. Items brought in could either be exchanged for cash or be converted to points. The points then can be a method of payment anywhere within the development to promote up-cycling goods. For this to be fully functional, a second story needs to be dedicated to sorting, tagging, and producing products for sale. Some items might go directly to resale while others may be used for materials. Items that don’t fit either category will be sent for appropriate recycling.

Other additions to our space will be education seminars for the community. This will include topics such as composting, community gardening, and recycling. These seminars will become a starting point for the community where one could learn how to support environmental sustainability in the surrounding community. Classes could range from how to maintain a community garden and how to properly dispose waste, to living with a smaller carbon footprint. This is to provide a strong sense of environmental sustainability throughout the development, from the people up to the development itself.

The last major method of curbing our carbon footprint is controlling how much energy is consumed and maintaining a micro-grid so unused energy is not going to waste. Items such as lights will operate off of solar power. They will charge during the day and then run at full capacity for part of the night. At around 10:30 or 11 pm the lights will switch to low energy modes to curb total energy consumption. The micro-grid will maintain and manage the total power coming off the grid into and out of the space pulling unused power back into the micro-grid to prevent waste. This will regulate our electric use and guarantee power stored from the solar panels is used before pulling from the Plano power grid.
